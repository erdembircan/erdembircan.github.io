Who Is Left to Hide an Easter Egg?

6/18/2026

6 minutes read

A Man Hides His Name

In 1979, a programmer named Warren Robinett hid his own name inside a video game, because the company he worked for would not let him put it on the box.

He was writing Adventure for the Atari 2600. Atari did not credit the people who made its games. A cartridge was made "by Atari," and the person who actually wrote it stayed anonymous, deliberately, so that no competitor could learn their name and hire them away. Robinett was paid around twenty-two thousand dollars a year. The game went on to sell roughly a million copies. His name appeared nowhere on it.

So he put it somewhere. To find it, you have to pick up an object that is invisible, a single gray dot buried in the catacombs of a castle, carry it to a wall that should not open, and walk through. Inside a hidden room, in slowly cycling colors, are three words: "Created by Warren Robinett." It is the only text in the entire game.

He was precise, years later, about what it was:

But I didn't call it an easter egg. I called it my signature. Like a painter would put down in the corner of his painting.

That is the whole thing, sitting right there in the first example the medium ever produced. Not a feature. Not a prank. A signature. A person insisting, quietly and against the rules, that a human being made this, and that the human being had a name.

The Name Was the Point

The term came later, and from someone else. A player found the room, wrote to Atari describing the impossible space, and the company decided it liked the surprise. An Atari manager, the story goes, said they should hide more of them, and started calling them Easter eggs. The label stuck and never came off. Atari kept Robinett's room in part because re-cutting the cartridge to remove it would have cost around ten thousand dollars.

It is worth being honest about the history, because the popular version flatters Robinett too much. He did not invent the hidden message. Programmers had been tucking their names and jokes into code for over a decade. There is a make love command from the late 1960s that answers back "not war?", and a 1977 arcade game with a hidden "Hi Ron!" left by its programmer. What Adventure added was not the gesture. It was the reason, made legible: a person, denied credit, taking it anyway.

That reason was human, not technical, and it was powerful enough to start a company. In 1979, four Atari programmers walked out and founded Activision, largely because Atari would neither name them nor pay them a share of games they had written by themselves. One of them, David Crane, had written games that earned Atari tens of millions while he took home around twenty thousand. The hidden room and the walkout were two answers to the same question. One was covert and one was a resignation letter, and both were a person refusing to be treated as interchangeable.

Taste, Smuggled In

Strip the romance and an Easter egg is just a choice nobody asked for. That is also the most interesting thing about it.

Microsoft Excel 97 contained a working flight simulator. Excel 95, two years earlier, hid something better: a Doom-style room called the Hall of Tortured Souls, with a wall displaying photographs of the actual developers. Sit with that. A group of people spent a year building a spreadsheet, and decided it should secretly contain their own faces. That is not an engineering decision. It is a point of view.

The genre is full of these small, unnecessary acts of taste. The Konami Code, up up down down left right left right B A, began as a testing cheat that Kazuhisa Hashimoto left in Gradius in 1986 and never removed, and became muscle memory for a generation. Donald Knuth numbers the versions of TeX so they converge on the digits of pi, and METAFONT so it converges on e, with the stated plan that the numbers freeze at those constants after his death, at which point any remaining bugs are to be reclassified as features. That is not a versioning scheme. It is a mathematician deciding his life's work should end on a joke that is also a tribute. There is an official HTTP status code, 418, that means "I'm a teapot," defined in an April Fools' document in 1998 and never fully evicted from the standard, because enough people had affection for it to keep a joke in the plumbing of the web on purpose.

None of these were required. That is exactly why they matter. They are the places where a person with taste made a choice they did not have to make, and signed the work by making it.

What We Sanded Off

The eggs mostly went away, and the reason was reasonable. After a bad run of security scares, Microsoft and others banned hidden, undocumented code outright. The logic is hard to argue with. You cannot ship the software in a hospital or an aircraft with a flight simulator nobody approved buried inside it. So the surprises were removed, software got cleaner and more professional and more auditable, and the fingerprints were sanded off on purpose.

I am not going to mourn the flight simulator in the spreadsheet. In a heart monitor, I want every line of code accounted for. The egg, as a literal artifact, earned its retirement.

What I want to talk about is the thing the egg was made of, because that is what is actually disappearing now, and it is disappearing for a completely different reason. Nobody is banning it. We are simply building a world that has no use for it.

Nobody Is Home

A large and growing share of the software written today is not written by a person at all. You describe what you want, a model produces something that works, and you ship it. It is fast, it is usually correct, and it has no point of view whatsoever.

This is the part that is hard to say without sounding like a crank, so let me be specific. When you read enough machine-generated code, or machine-generated copy, or machine-generated design, the thing you notice is that all of it is fine. It is competent and it is empty. It is the average of everything that has ever been written, regressed cleanly to the mean. It never makes a choice it did not have to make. It will never hide a flight simulator in a spreadsheet, and not because a policy forbids it. It will not because it has no reason to want to. It has no name it resents being left off the box. It has no taste it is desperate to express. It has nothing, anywhere, that it would bother to sign.

This is the connection I cannot stop seeing. Taste is the ability to make a choice that is not the obvious one and be right about it. Imagination is the willingness to make a choice nobody asked for. A model, by its nature, returns the most probable next thing, and the most probable thing is, by definition, the option with the least taste in it. We are filling our products with the average of all previous products, calling it productivity, and then acting puzzled that everything feels a little flat, a little interchangeable, a little like it was assembled by no one in particular. The flatness is not a bug in the technology. It is the technology working exactly as designed.

The Easter egg was never really about the hidden room. It was about a person who was told their work did not have an author and disagreed enough to do something about it. That impulse is the most human thing in all of software: pride, mischief, a grievance, and the sheer patience to bury something for a year and tell absolutely no one. Robinett kept his secret for a full year and did not even tell the people he shared an office with. A model has nothing it would keep, because a model has no self for the secret to belong to.

The Reason to Bother

You are reading this because you tapped a photo five times until this entire site turned into a forest. Nobody asked for that. It does not improve a single metric. It is here because a person thought it would be a good thing to hide, on the off chance that someone would go looking, and chose to spend an afternoon building it instead of doing something sensible.

That is the whole tradition, and it is also, more and more, the only part of this work that cannot be generated for you. A machine will happily write the code. What it cannot supply is the reason to put something here that nobody needed, simply because you have taste and you wanted to. Hold on to that part. Soon it may be the only proof left that a person was ever here at all.
